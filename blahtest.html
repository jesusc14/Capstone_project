Causes of Delays

There are over 200 bus routes that run in King County, however, of these bus routes most riders only take less than an eighth. So, the following graphs will study the 20 busiest and most important routes located in densely populated areas.

Terms used:
Average daily delay percentage, abbreviated to ADDP: The percentage that shows the average number of times a bus is late every day in 2022.
Correlation Coefficient: A correlation coefficient of zero indicates that no linear relationship exists between two continuous variables, and a correlation coefficient of −1 or +1 indicates a perfect linear relationship. The strength of a relationship can be anywhere between −1 and +1 [1].

1. Type of bus compared to ADDP

This graph shows the 20 busiest bus routes and compares their ADDP to the type of bus they are. There are three types of buses:

Conventional: a bus that runs entirely on gas.

Hybrid: a bus that is powered partially by an electric motor, and partially by a conventional engine.

Trolley: a bus powered by electricity from overhead wires.

The reason for these comparisons was to see if different bus types had a connection to delays. The reason for this is because it has been reported that different types of buses have differing problems that could cause delays. For instance, there are reported to be delays because of the overhead wires of a trolley bus disconnecting and needing time to be reconnected by mechanics. But for hybrid buses, it was reported to have a “24% lower rate of miles between all road calls than that of diesel buses” [2]. A road call is when a bus needs service because of a vehicle failure and causes a significant delay. So, in this instance hybrids seemed to have a positive effect on buses being on time. For conventional, or “diesel” buses, the reasons for most road calls were for “engine, doors and interlock, and cooling problems” [2]. But since these graphs are for researching the busiest bus routes, then there might be unexpected results. Of the 20 busiest bus routes, 5 were trolley, 5 were hybrid, and 10 were conventional. In fraction form ¼ were trolley, ¼ were hybrid, and ½ were conventional. The ADDP of all conventional buses added together and averaged is 16.4%. For hybrid this number is 19.2%, and for trolleys, this number is 15.2%.

This means that of the busiest bus routes, the ones with the most delays were:

1. Hybrid

2. Conventional

3. Trolley

2. Night owls compared to ADDP

This next graph shows the 20 busiest bus routes and compares whether they are a “night owl” or not to their ADDP.

A “night owl” is a bus that works at midnight from midnight to 5am in addition to their normal day hours. The reason for this comparison: It is less likely for a night owl to be late during late hours because of the absence of variables that would cause delays, such as traffic, the wait time it takes for bus riders to board during peak hours, less disruptions, etc. We will calculate whether this would affect the overall ADDP of a night owl vs a non-night owl.

Of the 20 bus routes, 9 of them are night owls, and 11 of them are not. The overall ADDP of night owls added together and average was 15.889%, whereas the non-night owls were 17.5455%. The non-night owls do have a general higher ADDP than night owls. When taking a night owl bus, especially at night, there is a higher likely chance it won’t come late.

3. Comparison between ADDP and the average amount of daily boardings in 2022.

This graph is a combination of two bar graphs, where the bottom one is of the ADDP of the 20 busiest bus routes, and the top one is of the amount of average boardings there is for each of the busiest routes in 2022. The top graph is in ascending order, where it starts with the buses that have the most daily boardings to the buses with the least daily boardings. The rapid ride bus lines are represented by green bars, and the rest by light blue.

The number of daily boardings could affect the punctuality of a bus for multiple reasons. If there are more riders taking a bus, the amount of boardings and offboarding could mean added time. The bus would also make more stops. If there are more riders it increases the chances of more bicyclists on the bus, which would in turn mean there are more bicycles being secured to and from the front of the bus. Increased riders on the bus also increases the chances of there being disruptions between riders, which depending on if reinforcements must be called would mean more delays for the other riders.

The comparison of these graphs shows that while the number of boarding decreases, the delays don’t seem to decrease with it, at least at first glance.

However, in the scatterplot version of this data above, there seems to be somewhat of a connection between these two variables, with both variables increasing. To see if there is a correlation between these two variables, we’ll have to find its correlation coefficient.

If the correlation coefficient is positive, then that means when one variable increases, the other increases with it, and when it is negative then when one increases the other decreases, and when it is zero or close to zero there is next to no correlation.

The correlation coefficient came out to roughly 0.2, which is a negligible correlation, meaning when one increases there is also a slight increase with the other but not by a lot. So, if a bus is busier (has more riders) than usual the chance that it will be delayed will slightly increase.

However, even when we look at the similar graph shown below that shows the routes of 139 buses to get a more general result, we could see that there still doesn’t seem to be any connection between these two variables. While the bar graph of daily boardings is descending, the bar graph for ADDP isn’t. The correlation coefficient between the ADDP and average amount of daily boardings of 139 bus routes is 0.304, which is a 0.1 increase from the same calculation we’ve done using 20 bus routes. A 0.3 correlation coefficient is considered to be a low positive correlation, which means that even when a large sample size is taken into account, the relationship between these two variables doesn’t change much.

4. Distance between the starting stop until the end stop compared to the ADDP.

This graph is just like graph three, except in place of the daily boarding variables is the distance between the start stop and the end stop in miles for each bus, with the rapid line routes in green and the rest of the routes in light blue.

The reason why the distance of a route could affect the punctuality of a bus could be explained depending on how one looks at it. We’ve already discussed that onboarding and offboarding adds time, so for a longer route it’s safe to say the increase in stops means these times add up more than a shorter route. Longer distances could also mean that a bus driver could get tired more and therefore could decrease the efficiency of their work.

Both the bar graph and the scatterplot (pictured above) don’t seem show much of a correlation between the two variables, but we will calculate the correlation coefficient for them to see if there is even a slight relationship. The correlation coefficient came out to -0.11, which means that when one variable increases, the other actually decreases. However, -0.11 is such a small number that it’s almost close to zero, which means it’s safe to say that there is no relationship between these two variables.

5. Number of stops for each bus compared to the ADDP

This graph is built the same as the third graph, except the top graph shows the number of stops for each bus. The reason why the number of stops could affect the punctuality of a bus is because of the onboarding and onboarding adding time. So, if a bus has more stops than another, then hypothetically these extra stops would cause it to be delayed more.

Both the bar graph and the scatterplot (pictured above) don’t seem to show any correlation between these two variables, and the correlation coefficient between the two is 0.075. This number is too close to zero to mean anything significant, which means there is no relationship between these two variables.

6. Route type compared to ADDP

This bar graph shows the number of ADDP’s each different route types have. The route type is the type of area each bus goes through, whether it’s suburban, urban, or rural and DART area. “DART is fixed-route transit service operated in King County under contract with Hopelink, using smaller transit vehicles with the flexibility to perform a limited number of off-route deviations upon request.” [3].

The area in which the route takes can affect the punctuality of a bus since different areas have more people than others, can be safer than other areas, could have less construction etc. Urban areas is known to have high population density, usually in downtown areas, or areas with large shopping malls and other commercial buildings. These routes would be more likely to go through places with more construction going on. Suburban areas are mostly places that are primarily residential, such as neighborhoods, alleyways, etc. with less commercial buildings like shops and gyms. The United States Department of Agriculture defines a rural area as an “open countryside with population densities less than 500 people per square mile and places with fewer than 2,500 people.”

Of the 139 bus routes, the ones that go through an urban area seem to have the most delays. However, this bar graph doesn’t show a fair picture since most bus routes are situated in an urban area, therefore making it seem like they have more delays. To solve this, we will average all the ADDP’s of each area buses and see which one has the highest number.

This next graph shows just that. For the urban area it’s average ADDP was 12.7, for suburban it was 11.2, and for rural and dart it was 17. That means the route types with the worst delays are rural and dart, then urban, then suburban.

Summary

• Of the busiest bus routes, the types of buses with the most delays are hybrid, conventional, and trolley in that order.
• Non-night owl buses on average have more delays than a night owl bus.
• If a bus has more riders, then the chance that it will be late slightly increases.
• The total length of a route does not affect its punctuality.
• The total number of stops on a route does not affect its punctuality.
• The route types with the worst delays are rural and dart, urban, and suburban, in that order.
